Instructions

Bring a large pot of water to a boil and add 1 tablespoon of salt. Cook your linguine until 1-2 minutes *before* al dente. Reserve at least 2 cups of the cloudy pasta water before draining.

Pat the shrimp dry with paper towels (moisture is the enemy of searing). Season with salt and pepper. In a large skillet, heat oil and 1 tbsp butter over medium-high heat until the butter foams.

Sear the shrimp for 1-2 minutes per side until they turn pink and form a 'C' shape. Remove them immediately from the pan and set aside on a plate to prevent overcooking.

Lower the heat to medium. In the same pan, add another tablespoon of butter and sauté the garlic and pepper flakes for 60 seconds. Deglaze with white wine and lemon juice, scraping up any browned bits.

Add the undercooked pasta to the skillet along with 1/2 cup of pasta water. Increase heat and toss vigorously. Slowly add the remaining *cold* butter pieces while stirring—this creates the creamy emulsion.

Return the shrimp and their juices to the pan. Add parsley and lemon zest. Toss for another 30 seconds until the sauce is glossy and coats the pasta perfectly. Serve immediately with extra zest.

You Must Know

The secret to "creamy" shrimp pasta without cream is the Pasta Water Emulsion. Never drain your pasta fully! The starch in the water acts as a bridge between the fat (butter) and the acid (wine/lemon). When you toss them together over high heat, they bind into a silky glaze. If your sauce looks oily or separated, you need more pasta water and more vigorous tossing!

Storage Tips

Seafood pasta is notorious for being best consumed fresh. The butter sauce will solidify and the shrimp will lose their tenderness if refrigerated and reheated incorrectly. However, you can keep leftovers in an airtight container for up to 24 hours.

To reheat: Do NOT use a microwave! Place the pasta in a skillet with 2-3 tablespoons of water or chicken broth. Warm over the lowest heat possible, stirring constantly to re-emulsify the sauce. Remove from heat the moment it's warm to avoid rubbery shrimp.

Ingredient Substitutions

  • The Wine: If you avoid alcohol, substitute with chicken stock or clam juice mixed with an extra teaspoon of lemon juice.
  • The Protein: Sea scallops or sliced calamari work beautifully with this exact same sauce base.
  • The Butter: For a dairy-free version, use a high-quality vegan butter block, but ensure it's cold when added at the end.
  • Linguine: Fettuccine or even Angel Hair (Capellini) are great alternatives. Adjust cooking time for Angel Hair as it's very thin.
  • Red Pepper Flakes: Use a pinch of Cayenne or freshly diced red chili if you want more localized heat.

Serving Suggestions

This dish is bright and rich, so it needs simple, crisp accompaniments to balance the palate:

  • Serve with a side of charred broccolini or roasted asparagus with a squeeze of lemon
  • A simple arugula salad with shaved Parmesan and a light balsamic vinaigrette
  • Fresh, crusty sourdough or garlic bread to mop up the extra lemon-butter sauce
  • Pair with the same crisp white wine you used for cooking (Pinot Grigio is perfect)
  • Top with a small handful of toasted breadcrumbs (pangrattato) for a delightful crunch

Freezer Meal Conversion

Because of the delicate nature of cooked shrimp and butter emulsions, this dish does not freeze well once fully assembled. However, you can prep components:

  • Marinated Shrimp: Mix raw shrimp with garlic, olive oil, and lemon zest. Freeze in a vacuum bag for up to 2 months.
  • Lemon-Garlic Butter: Mix softened butter with garlic, parsley, and lemon zest. Freeze in ice cube trays for an instant sauce starter.
  • The Meal: We recommend making the pasta fresh—it only takes 20 minutes and the texture difference is massive.

Pro Tips

  • Dry your shrimp: If they are wet, they will steam instead of searing. Paper towels are your best friend here.
  • Cold Butter: Use cold butter for the emulsion (Step 05). Cold fat incorporates into the hot liquid more slowly and stably than warm fat.
  • Pasta Salt: Your pasta water should taste "like the sea." This is your only chance to season the heart of the pasta.
  • Zest vs. Juice: Never use bottled lemon juice. The zest contains the aromatic oils that provide the 'scent' of lemon, while the juice provides the 'acid'. You need both.
  • Microplane: Use a microplane for the garlic to create a paste; it melts into the sauce without leaving chunky bits.
